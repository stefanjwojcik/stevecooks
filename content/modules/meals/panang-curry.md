# Panang Curry

Panang curry is Thailand's richest, most indulgent curry — thick, creamy, and fragrant with kaffir lime, galangal, and lemongrass. Unlike the thinner red or green curries, Panang is almost sauce-like in consistency, with a deep peanut undertone and a mild-to-medium heat. It's a perfect pescatarian meal built around tofu, shrimp, or a firm white fish.

## Panang vs. Other Thai Curries

| Curry | Consistency | Heat | Key Flavor |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Panang** | Thick, creamy | Mild–medium | Peanut, kaffir lime |
| **Red curry** | Medium | Medium–hot | Chili, galangal |
| **Green curry** | Thin, brothy | Hot | Fresh chili, basil |
| **Massaman** | Thick | Mild | Potato, peanut, warm spice |
| **Yellow curry** | Medium | Mild | Turmeric, cumin |

Panang paste contains dried chilies, lemongrass, galangal, shrimp paste (in traditional versions), kaffir lime zest, and roasted peanuts. **For a fully pescatarian Panang**, use a brand without shrimp paste, or make it from scratch.

## Choosing Your Protein

- **Tofu** — firm or extra-firm, pressed and pan-fried first for texture
- **Shrimp** — large, shell-off; add at the end (overcooks quickly)
- **Halibut or cod** — cut into chunks; gently poach in the curry
- **Tempeh** — slice and pan-fry; holds up well to the rich sauce

## The Recipe

**Serves 3–4 | Ready in 30 minutes**

### Ingredients

- 2 tbsp coconut oil or neutral oil
- 3–4 tbsp Panang curry paste (Mae Ploy or Maesri brands are excellent)
- 2 cans (13.5 oz each) full-fat coconut milk, divided
- 1 tbsp palm sugar or brown sugar
- 2 tbsp fish sauce (use soy sauce or tamari for vegan)
- 4 kaffir lime leaves, torn (or 1 tsp lime zest if unavailable)
- 1 lb protein of choice (firm tofu, shrimp, halibut)
- 1 red bell pepper, thinly sliced
- 1 cup Thai basil leaves (or Italian basil)
- Steamed jasmine rice, for serving
- 1–2 red chilies, thinly sliced (for garnish)
- 2 tbsp roasted peanuts, roughly chopped (optional)

### Method

#### 1. Fry the Paste
Heat oil in a wok or heavy saucepan over medium heat. Add curry paste and fry, stirring constantly, for 1–2 minutes until fragrant and darkened slightly. This step is essential — cooking the paste in oil unlocks its aromatics.

#### 2. Add Coconut Cream
Pour in just the thick cream from the top of one can of coconut milk (don't shake the can). Stir into the paste and cook 2–3 minutes, stirring, until the mixture "splits" — you'll see orange oil start to separate from the sauce. This is a good sign; it means the fat is carrying the flavor.

#### 3. Build the Curry
Add the remaining coconut milk (including the rest of the first can). Add sugar, fish sauce, and torn kaffir lime leaves. Bring to a gentle simmer.

#### 4. Add Vegetables and Protein
- Add bell pepper and cook 3 minutes.
- **For tofu or fish:** add now and simmer 5–7 minutes until cooked through.
- **For shrimp:** add in the last 3–4 minutes only.

#### 5. Taste and Adjust
Panang is all about balance. Taste and adjust:
- **Too sweet?** Add a few more drops of fish sauce or lime juice
- **Too salty?** Add a pinch of sugar
- **Too thick?** Add a splash of water or more coconut milk
- **More heat?** Add a spoonful more paste

#### 6. Finish and Serve
Remove from heat. Tear in Thai basil leaves. Serve over jasmine rice, garnished with sliced chilies, peanuts, and a squeeze of lime.

## The "Split" Technique

Authentic Thai curries use the "split" (or "crack") technique where coconut cream separates into oil and solids as it fries with the paste. Here's what's happening:

- Coconut cream contains ~20% fat
- When cooked down without added water, the fat separates out
- The curry paste fries in this fat rather than water-sautés — creating a much deeper flavor

To get a proper split: **use full-fat coconut milk, don't shake the can, and use the thickest cream from the top.**

## Make-Ahead Tips

- The curry base (without protein) keeps refrigerated for 3 days and freezes well
- Add fresh protein when reheating — especially shrimp, which toughens when reheated
- Panang thickens as it sits; thin with a splash of coconut milk when reheating

## Key Tips

1. **Full-fat coconut milk only** — light coconut milk won't split properly and produces a watery curry
2. **Fry the paste** — this is non-negotiable; raw paste tastes raw
3. **Kaffir lime leaves** — don't skip these if you can find them; they're what makes Panang distinctive
4. **Add basil off heat** — heat wilts and blackens Thai basil quickly; stir in at the very end
5. **Season with fish sauce, not salt** — fish sauce adds umami depth that salt alone can't replicate
