# The Sourdough Starter

A sourdough starter is a living culture of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria, fed on flour and water. It's the leavener for sourdough bread, and it's what gives sourdough its characteristic tang, complex flavor, and chewy texture. Building one from scratch takes 7–14 days — but once you have an active starter, you have it for life.

## What's Actually Happening

Wild yeast and bacteria are naturally present on flour and in the air. When you mix flour and water and leave it at room temperature, these organisms colonize the mixture:

- **Wild yeast** (mainly *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* and *Kazachstania* species) produce CO₂ — the gas that makes bread rise
- **Lactic acid bacteria** (*Lactobacillus* species) produce lactic and acetic acids — the sour flavor and extended shelf life

The bacteria acidify the environment, which keeps harmful pathogens out and creates the right conditions for the yeast to thrive. It's a self-regulating ecosystem.

## What You Need

- A clean glass jar (quart-sized mason jar works perfectly)
- Kitchen scale
- Whole wheat or rye flour (for establishing; the extra nutrients help)
- All-purpose or bread flour (for ongoing maintenance)
- Unchlorinated water (filtered or left out overnight to off-gas chlorine)
- A rubber band or piece of tape to mark the level

## The 7-Day Process

### Days 1–2: Starting From Scratch

**Day 1:**
In a clean jar, mix:
- 50g whole wheat or rye flour
- 50g water (room temperature)

Stir vigorously — this incorporates oxygen, which the yeast needs early on. Cover loosely (not airtight — the culture needs airflow) and leave at room temperature (70–75°F ideally).

**Day 2:**
You may see small bubbles — or nothing yet. That's normal. **Discard** half the starter (50g) and feed:
- 25g whole wheat flour
- 25g all-purpose flour
- 50g water

### Days 3–4: Activity Picks Up

By day 3, you should see more bubbles, some rise, and possibly a tangy or cheesy smell. The starter may smell quite funky at this stage — that's okay; it's just the early bacteria competing for dominance.

**Discard and feed daily:**
- Discard all but 50g of starter
- Add 50g flour + 50g water

The discard at this stage should not be used for baking — the culture isn't established enough.

### Days 5–7: Finding Rhythm

By day 5, a healthy starter will:
- Double in size 4–8 hours after feeding
- Have a domed or slightly collapsed top when at peak
- Smell pleasantly sour and yeasty (like beer or yogurt, not nail polish remover)
- Show a network of bubbles throughout

**Transition to all-purpose or bread flour** by day 5 if you started with whole wheat.

### The Float Test

To check if your starter is active enough to bake with: drop a small spoonful into a glass of water. If it floats, there's enough gas production — it's ready. If it sinks, keep feeding.

## Understanding Your Starter's Cycle

After each feeding, your starter goes through a predictable arc:

```
Feed → Lag phase → Rise (doubling) → Peak → Collapse (hooch forms)
```

| Phase | Time After Feeding | What You See |
|---|---|---|
| **Lag** | 0–2 hours | Little activity; starter is "waking up" |
| **Rise** | 2–6 hours | Bubbles forming; starter expanding |
| **Peak** | 4–8 hours | Maximum height; domed top; most active |
| **Fall** | 6–12 hours | Starts to collapse; gray liquid may form |

**Bake at peak** — this is when the starter has the most yeast activity and leavening power.

## Hooch: What It Is and What It Means

If you see a gray or dark liquid layer on top of your starter, that's **hooch** — alcohol produced by the yeast when the starter has exhausted its food supply. It's harmless. It means your starter is hungry.

- **Stir it back in** if the starter just smells tangy
- **Pour it off** if the smell is very sharp or alcoholic
- Then feed as normal

A hooch-forming starter is not dead — it just needs more frequent feeding.

## Ongoing Maintenance

### Room Temperature (Active Use)
Feed once or twice daily. Use 1:1:1 ratio — 1 part starter : 1 part flour : 1 part water (by weight).

**Example:** 50g starter + 50g flour + 50g water

### Refrigerator (Long-Term Storage)
Once established, you can refrigerate your starter and feed it weekly. Take it out, let it come to room temperature, feed it, let it peak, then use or return to the fridge.

A refrigerated starter can go weeks between feedings without dying — though it will need 1–2 feedings to become fully active again before baking.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| No activity after 5 days | Too cold; chlorinated water | Move to warmer spot; use filtered water |
| Pink or orange streaks | Contamination | Discard and start over |
| Smells like nail polish remover | Ethyl acetate; stressed culture | More frequent feedings; adjust temp |
| Smells very sour/acidic | Over-fermented | Feed more frequently or use higher flour ratio |
| Slow to rise | Weak culture; cold temperature | Feed 2x/day; move to warmer spot |

## The Discard

The flour and starter you remove during feedings is called **discard**. Once your starter is established (Day 5+), discard is perfectly usable and actually tastes great:

- **Pancakes and waffles** — adds tang and lift
- **Crackers** — crispy, complex flavor
- **Pizza dough** — great texture
- **Banana bread or muffins** — adds depth

Don't throw it away — collect it in a container in the fridge and bake with it throughout the week.

## Key Tips

1. **Weigh everything** — the ratio of flour to water matters; measuring by volume introduces inconsistency
2. **Temperature is the biggest variable** — a cold kitchen (below 68°F) will dramatically slow your starter; find the warmest spot in your home
3. **Be patient** — some starters take 10–14 days to fully activate; if you see some activity, keep going
4. **Consistency matters** — feed at the same time each day; your starter will adapt to a schedule
5. **Name it** — longtime bakers always name their starters; it makes the feeding feel less like a chore
