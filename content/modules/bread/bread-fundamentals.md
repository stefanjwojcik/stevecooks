# Bread Baking Fundamentals

Bread is one of the oldest foods in the world, and understanding it changes how you cook everything. At its core, bread is four ingredients — flour, water, salt, and a leavener — but the interactions between them are rich and learnable. This lesson covers the science you need before you bake your first loaf.

## The Four Ingredients

### Flour
Flour provides structure. When flour is mixed with water, two proteins (glutenin and gliadin) bond together to form **gluten** — the elastic network that gives bread its chew and traps the gas that makes bread rise.

| Flour Type | Protein % | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Bread flour** | 12–14% | Artisan loaves, sourdough, bagels |
| **All-purpose flour** | 10–12% | Most home baking; versatile |
| **Whole wheat flour** | 13–14% | Dense, nutty loaves; use in combination |
| **Pastry / cake flour** | 7–9% | Tender baked goods; not for bread |

More protein = more gluten = chewier, stronger bread. Bread flour produces a better open crumb in artisan loaves; all-purpose is fine for most recipes.

### Water
Water hydrates the flour proteins (creating gluten) and activates yeast. The ratio of water to flour is called **hydration** and is expressed as a percentage.

- **60–65% hydration** — Stiff dough; easy to shape; dense crumb (baguette-style)
- **70–75% hydration** — Moderate; good for most home loaves
- **80%+ hydration** — Wet, sticky, open crumb (ciabatta, high-hydration sourdough)

Higher hydration = more open, irregular crumb and crispier crust — but harder to handle.

### Salt
Salt does three things:
1. **Flavors** the bread (bread without salt tastes flat)
2. **Strengthens gluten** — tightens the dough structure
3. **Controls fermentation** — slows yeast activity, preventing over-fermentation

Standard salt percentage: **2% of flour weight** (e.g., 10g salt per 500g flour). Do not add salt directly onto yeast — it can inhibit or kill it.

### Leavener
The gas that makes bread rise comes from:

| Leavener | How It Works | Time | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Commercial yeast** | Releases CO₂ quickly | 1–3 hours | Everyday loaves, pizza |
| **Sourdough starter** | Wild yeast + bacteria; slow fermentation | 4–24+ hours | Artisan loaves, complex flavor |
| **Baking powder** | Chemical reaction; instant | Minutes | Quick breads, muffins |
| **Baking soda + acid** | Chemical reaction; instant | Minutes | Soda bread, pancakes |

## Gluten Development

Gluten is built in two ways: **mechanical** (kneading) and **time** (autolyse and bulk fermentation).

### Kneading
Traditional kneading stretches and folds the dough repeatedly, aligning gluten strands into a strong network. A properly kneaded dough:
- Is smooth and elastic
- Springs back slowly when poked
- Passes the **windowpane test**: stretch a small piece thin enough to see light through without tearing

### The Windowpane Test
Pinch a golf-ball piece of dough. Slowly stretch it between your fingers. If you can stretch it into a thin, translucent membrane without it tearing, gluten is fully developed. If it tears immediately, keep kneading.

### Stretch and Fold (No-Knead Method)
Instead of kneading, you can develop gluten passively through time and periodic stretching. Every 30 minutes during bulk fermentation, grab one side of the dough, stretch it up, and fold it over itself. Rotate 90° and repeat — 4 folds = 1 set. Do 3–4 sets over 2 hours.

## The Bread-Making Process

### 1. Mix (Autolyse)
Combine flour and water; rest 20–60 minutes. Gluten begins forming without any kneading — the dough becomes smoother and easier to work.

### 2. Add Salt and Leavener
Add salt (and yeast or starter) and incorporate fully.

### 3. Bulk Fermentation
The dough rests and rises at room temperature. Yeast produces CO₂; the dough develops flavor. This is where most of the character of your bread is built.

### 4. Shape
Gently degas, then shape into a boule (round) or batard (oval). Good shaping creates surface tension that helps the loaf hold structure in the oven.

### 5. Proof
The shaped loaf rests again — either at room temperature (1–2 hours) or in the refrigerator overnight (cold proof). Cold proofing slows fermentation and develops flavor.

### 6. Score
Just before baking, score the top of the loaf with a sharp blade (lame or razor). This controls where the bread expands in the oven.

### 7. Bake
High heat (450–500°F) is essential. Steam in the first phase keeps the crust soft so the loaf can expand; the second phase (dry heat) sets the crust and creates color.

**Dutch oven method:** Preheat a Dutch oven in the oven. Bake covered for 20 minutes (steam is trapped inside), then uncover for 20–25 minutes. This replicates a professional steam-injected oven and is the best method for home bakers.

## Understanding Fermentation

Fermentation is the heart of bread flavor. Yeast consumes sugars in the flour and produces CO₂ (which makes bread rise) and ethanol (which evaporates in the oven). Bacteria also produce organic acids that create complex, slightly tangy flavors — especially in sourdough.

**Temperature controls speed:**
- Warmer = faster fermentation (less flavor development)
- Cooler = slower fermentation (more time = more flavor)

Ideal bulk fermentation temp: **75–78°F**. Below 65°F, fermentation slows dramatically. Above 85°F, it accelerates and can over-ferment.

## Key Tips

1. **Weigh, don't measure** — baking is precise; use a kitchen scale for flour and water
2. **Flour type matters** — substituting all-purpose for bread flour changes the result; use what the recipe calls for
3. **Temperature matters** — the temperature of your water affects fermentation speed; 70–80°F water is ideal for most loaves
4. **Don't rush fermentation** — under-fermented bread is dense and gummy; over-fermented bread is slack and sour
5. **Score deeply and confidently** — a timid score results in bread that "blows out" on the side instead of opening at the cut
