# Focaccia

Focaccia is the most forgiving, most rewarding bread a home baker can make. It requires no shaping skill, no Dutch oven, and no special equipment — just a sheet pan, plenty of olive oil, and a little patience. The result is a thick, pillowy flatbread with a crispy, olive oil-soaked bottom and dimpled top. It's the best entry point into yeast bread, and it scales directly into understanding sourdough.

## What Makes Great Focaccia

Three things define great focaccia:
1. **High hydration dough** — wetter than almost any other bread (80%+), which creates the open, airy texture
2. **Generous olive oil** — both in the dough and pooled in the pan; this is not the place to be shy
3. **Cold fermentation** — an overnight rest in the fridge develops extraordinary flavor from very little effort

## Focaccia vs. Pizza Dough

Both use flour, water, yeast, salt, and olive oil — but focaccia has:
- Higher hydration (~80% vs. 60–65% for pizza)
- More olive oil
- A longer, slower proof
- No shaping — it's stretched directly in the pan

Once you can make focaccia, pizza dough feels simple.

## The Recipe

**Makes one 9×13-inch pan**

### Ingredients

**Dough:**
- 500g bread flour (or all-purpose)
- 400g lukewarm water (80°F)
- 7g instant yeast (1 packet) or 10g active dry yeast
- 10g kosher salt
- 50g olive oil, plus more for the pan and finishing

**For the pan:**
- 50g olive oil (generously coat the pan)

**Toppings:**
- Flaky sea salt (Maldon or similar)
- Fresh rosemary sprigs
- Optional: olives, cherry tomatoes, thinly sliced red onion, roasted garlic

### Method

#### Day 1: Mix and Cold Proof (15 minutes active)

**1. Bloom the yeast (if using active dry)**
Stir yeast into 400g of water with a pinch of sugar. Wait 5–10 minutes until foamy. If using instant yeast, skip this step and add directly to flour.

**2. Mix the dough**
In a large bowl, combine flour, salt, yeast mixture, and 50g olive oil. Stir with a fork or your hands until no dry flour remains. The dough will be very wet and sticky — that's correct. Do not add more flour.

**3. Fold and rest**
Over the next hour, perform 3–4 sets of stretch-and-folds (every 20 minutes). The dough will become noticeably smoother and more elastic with each set.

**4. Cold proof**
Drizzle a little olive oil over the top of the dough, cover the bowl tightly with plastic wrap, and refrigerate for **18–24 hours**. This is where the flavor develops. The longer, the better (up to 72 hours).

#### Day 2: Pan and Bake (20 minutes active + 2 hours rest)

**5. Oil the pan**
Pour 50g (about ¼ cup) of olive oil into a 9×13-inch rimmed baking sheet. Spread it to coat the entire surface, including the sides.

**6. Transfer and rest**
Turn the cold dough onto the oiled pan. Gently fold it over itself once, then flip so the oiled side is up. Don't stretch yet — let it rest 45 minutes to 1 hour at room temperature. The gluten needs to relax before you can stretch it.

**7. Dimple and stretch**
Using all ten fingers, press into the dough firmly, pushing it toward the edges of the pan. Don't be timid — press all the way down. If the dough resists, cover and wait 15 more minutes. The signature focaccia dimples should be deep (½ to ¾ inch).

**8. Add toppings and final proof**
Drizzle generously with more olive oil (it should pool in the dimples — this is right). Press rosemary sprigs and other toppings in. Sprinkle flaky salt. Let rest uncovered 30–45 minutes.

**9. Bake**
Preheat oven to **450°F (230°C)**. Bake for 20–25 minutes until deep golden brown on top. Check the bottom by lifting a corner with a spatula — it should be a rich, even brown. If the top colors before the bottom is done, move the pan to the lowest rack for the last few minutes.

**10. Cool**
Transfer immediately to a wire rack to prevent steam from softening the bottom. Rest 15–20 minutes before cutting.

## The Olive Oil Rules

There is no such thing as too much olive oil in focaccia. The oil:
- Creates the crispy, almost fried bottom
- Keeps the crumb moist for days
- Carries flavor from toppings into the bread

If the oil in the pan looks alarming, you're probably doing it right.

## Same-Day Focaccia (Faster Version)

If you don't have time for an overnight proof:
- Use the same recipe with warm water (90°F)
- After mixing and folding, proof at room temperature 1.5–2 hours (until doubled)
- Then pan, dimple, top, and bake

The flavor won't be as complex, but it will still be excellent — better than most bakeries.

## Sourdough Focaccia

Substitute your sourdough starter for commercial yeast:
- Replace yeast with 150g active starter
- Reduce water to 350g
- Extend the cold proof to 24–36 hours
- Everything else stays the same

The result has a beautiful tang and keeps longer than yeasted focaccia.

## Storing and Reheating

- **Room temperature:** Best the day it's made; good for 2 days wrapped in a towel (not plastic — the crust softens)
- **Reheat:** 5 minutes in a 400°F oven on a wire rack; the bottom crisps right back up
- **Freeze:** Cut into squares, wrap individually; freeze up to 1 month; reheat from frozen at 400°F for 10 minutes

## Key Tips

1. **Don't skimp on the oil in the pan** — the bottom should essentially fry in olive oil as it bakes
2. **Let the dough relax before stretching** — if it springs back, cover and wait; forcing it tears the gluten structure
3. **Dimple deeply and confidently** — shallow dimples puff back up; press all the way through
4. **The longer the cold proof, the better the flavor** — 24 hours is better than 18; 48 is better than 24
5. **Check the bottom, not just the top** — focaccia should be evenly brown underneath, not just golden on top
