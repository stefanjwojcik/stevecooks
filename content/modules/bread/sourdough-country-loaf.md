# Sourdough Country Loaf

This is the classic open-crumb sourdough boule — the bread that defined the modern artisan bread movement. It's made with mostly bread flour and a small amount of whole wheat for nutty depth. The process takes two days (a long cold proof overnight), but the active work is only about 30 minutes. The result is a crackling crust, an open, chewy crumb, and a depth of flavor that commercial yeast simply cannot replicate.

## Before You Begin

You need an **active, bubbly starter** that has been fed within the last 4–8 hours and is at or near peak activity. If your starter has been in the fridge, take it out the day before, feed it, and let it peak at room temperature before using.

**Tools you'll need:**
- Kitchen scale (essential)
- Large mixing bowl
- Bench scraper
- Banneton (proofing basket) or a bowl lined with a floured kitchen towel
- Dutch oven (5 qt or larger)
- Razor blade, lame, or very sharp serrated knife for scoring

## The Recipe

**Makes 1 loaf (about 900g)**

### Ingredients

- 450g bread flour
- 50g whole wheat flour
- 370g water, divided (350g for dough + 20g for adding salt)
- 100g active sourdough starter (at peak)
- 10g kosher salt

**Hydration:** ~73%

### Timeline (Two-Day Schedule)

| Time | Step |
|---|---|
| Morning, Day 1 | Feed starter |
| Afternoon, Day 1 | Mix dough, autolyse |
| Late afternoon | Add starter + salt; bulk fermentation begins |
| Every 30 min x4 | Stretch and fold sets |
| Evening | Shape; place in banneton |
| Refrigerate overnight | Cold proof (8–14 hours) |
| Morning, Day 2 | Preheat oven + Dutch oven; score and bake |

---

### Method

#### Step 1: Autolyse (30 minutes)
In a large bowl, mix 450g bread flour, 50g whole wheat flour, and 350g water until no dry flour remains. The dough will be shaggy and rough. Cover and rest 30–60 minutes. During this rest, gluten forms without any effort.

#### Step 2: Add Starter
Add 100g of your active, peaked starter to the autolysed dough. Using wet hands, squeeze and fold the starter through the dough until fully incorporated. It will feel messy at first — keep working until the dough is cohesive.

#### Step 3: Add Salt
Dissolve 10g of salt in the remaining 20g of water. Pour over the dough and incorporate the same way — squeeze, fold, and stretch until the salt is fully distributed and no water pools remain.

#### Step 4: Bulk Fermentation (4–5 hours at 75–78°F)
Cover the bowl and leave at room temperature. This is the most important phase. The dough should increase in volume by 50–75%.

**Stretch and Fold Sets (first 2 hours):**
Every 30 minutes for the first 2 hours, perform one set:
1. Wet one hand; grab one side of the dough
2. Stretch it up as high as it will go without tearing
3. Fold it over to the opposite side
4. Rotate the bowl 90° and repeat — 4 folds = 1 set

After 4 sets (2 hours), leave the dough undisturbed for the remaining 2–3 hours.

**How to know bulk fermentation is done:**
- Dough has grown 50–75%
- Surface is domed and smooth with bubbles visible at the edges
- Dough jiggles when you shake the bowl
- A small piece floats when dropped in water

#### Step 5: Pre-shape
Turn the dough gently onto an unfloured surface. Using a bench scraper in one hand and your other hand, drag the dough toward you in a circular motion, building surface tension. Let it rest uncovered for 20–30 minutes (bench rest).

#### Step 6: Final Shape (Boule)
Lightly flour your work surface. Flip the dough and gently stretch it into a rough rectangle. Fold the left third to center, right third over that, then roll the top toward you tightly. Flip seam-side down and drag the scraper under it to create tension. The surface should feel taut.

**Place seam-side up in a floured banneton** (use a 1:1 mix of rice flour and all-purpose flour to prevent sticking). Rice flour is key — it doesn't absorb moisture and won't glue the dough to the basket.

#### Step 7: Cold Proof
Cover the banneton with a shower cap or plastic wrap and refrigerate overnight (8–14 hours). Cold fermentation slows yeast activity and develops the most complex flavor. You can refrigerate for up to 24 hours if needed.

#### Step 8: Bake
Place your Dutch oven (with lid) in the oven. Preheat to **500°F (260°C)** for at least 45 minutes — the Dutch oven must be screaming hot.

Remove the dough from the refrigerator. Cut a piece of parchment paper to fit your Dutch oven. Flip the cold dough onto the parchment (seam-side down now).

**Score immediately:** Use a razor blade or lame held at a 30–45° angle. Make one confident slash across the top, about ¾ inch deep. A steep angle produces "ear" (a dramatic raised flap); a flatter angle creates a wider opening.

Carefully lower the dough + parchment into the hot Dutch oven. Cover and bake:
- **Covered: 20 minutes at 500°F** (steam trapped inside helps the loaf spring up)
- **Uncover: reduce to 450°F, bake 20–25 minutes** until deep brown

**The loaf is done when it sounds hollow when tapped on the bottom.** Internal temperature should be 205–210°F.

#### Step 9: Cool Completely
Transfer to a wire rack. **Wait at least 1 hour before cutting.** The crumb is still cooking internally as steam escapes. Cutting too early produces a gummy, wet crumb even if the bread is otherwise perfect.

## Reading Your Bake

### The Crumb
- **Open, irregular holes** — proper bulk fermentation, high hydration
- **Dense, tight crumb** — under-fermented, or dough was degassed too much during shaping
- **Large holes at top, dense at bottom** — over-fermented

### The Crust
- **Thick, shattering crust** — properly steamed and fully baked; the sign of a great loaf
- **Pale, soft crust** — underbaked; return to oven uncovered for 5–10 more minutes
- **Very dark/burnt bottom** — Dutch oven too hot; place a baking sheet under it next time

### The Ear
The "ear" is the raised flap that forms along the score line. It's the sign of a well-scored, properly fermented loaf with good oven spring.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Flat loaf, no oven spring | Under-fermented; weak starter | Extend bulk fermentation; feed starter more |
| Dough spreads flat when shaped | Over-fermented; too wet | Shorten bulk time; reduce hydration to 68% |
| Gummy crumb | Under-baked; cut too soon | Bake longer; cool 2 hours before cutting |
| No ear on score | Blade angle too steep; dough over-proofed | Score at 30° angle; reduce proof time |
| Crust softens after cooling | Normal for high-humidity environments | Leave uncovered at room temperature |

## Variations

### Country Loaf with Seeds
Add 50g of sesame, poppy, or sunflower seeds after the autolyse. Press seeds onto the outside of the shaped loaf before proofing.

### Higher Whole Wheat
Replace 100g of the bread flour with whole wheat flour (20% whole wheat). The dough will feel slightly stickier; reduce hydration to 360g water.

### Batard (Oval Loaf)
Shape the dough into an oval instead of a round for a different presentation. A batard has a higher ratio of crust to crumb and a more dramatic score.

## Key Tips

1. **Cold dough is easier to score** — pulling the dough straight from the refrigerator makes scoring clean and precise
2. **Preheat the Dutch oven long enough** — 45 minutes minimum; a cold Dutch oven will ruin oven spring
3. **Rice flour in the banneton** — all-purpose flour absorbs moisture and sticks; rice flour does not
4. **Don't rush the cool** — an hour minimum; ideally 2; the bread is not done cooking when it comes out of the oven
5. **Every loaf teaches you something** — take notes; sourdough is a skill built across dozens of bakes, not one
