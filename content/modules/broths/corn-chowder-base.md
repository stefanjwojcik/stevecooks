# Corn Chowder Base

Corn chowder lives or dies by its broth. The secret is making **corn stock from the cobs** — stripping the kernels and simmering the bare cobs in liquid extracts a milky, intensely sweet corn flavor that no store-bought broth can replicate. This base is creamy without being heavy, and it tastes unmistakably of summer corn.

## Why This Base Works

Most chowders rely entirely on heavy cream for body. This base uses a different approach:

- **Corn cob stock** — the stripped cobs are simmered to release their starch and natural sugars
- **Potato starch** — a small amount of potato, cooked into the base, thickens it naturally
- **Aromatics built in butter** — leek and celery create a gentle, savory backbone

The result is a broth that's thick, sweet, and rich without feeling dense.

## The Base Recipe

**Makes enough for one large pot of chowder (serves 6)**

### Ingredients

**For the corn stock:**
- 4–6 corn cobs (kernels stripped and reserved separately)
- 4 cups water
- 1 cup whole milk
- 1 bay leaf
- ½ tsp kosher salt

**For the chowder base:**
- 3 tbsp unsalted butter
- 1 large leek, white and light green parts, sliced and rinsed
- 2 celery stalks, sliced thin
- 3 garlic cloves, minced
- ¼ cup all-purpose flour
- 1 lb Yukon Gold potatoes, peeled and cut into ½-inch cubes
- 1 tsp fresh thyme leaves (or ½ tsp dried)
- Reserved corn kernels (from 4–6 ears)
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp white pepper
- ½ cup heavy cream (or half-and-half for a lighter version)

### Method

#### 1. Make the Corn Stock
Snap or cut the stripped cobs in half. Combine cobs, water, milk, bay leaf, and salt in a large pot. Bring to a boil, reduce heat, and simmer uncovered for 20–25 minutes. The liquid will turn pale yellow and taste distinctly of corn. Strain and discard cobs. You should have about 4 cups of corn stock.

#### 2. Build the Base
In a Dutch oven or heavy pot, melt butter over medium heat. Add leek and celery. Cook 6–8 minutes until softened and translucent, without browning. Add garlic and thyme; cook 1 minute.

#### 3. Make the Roux
Sprinkle flour over the vegetables and stir to coat. Cook 2 minutes, stirring constantly — this cooks out the raw flour taste. The mixture will look pasty and clumpy; that's correct.

#### 4. Add the Stock and Potatoes
Gradually pour in the corn stock, whisking as you go to prevent lumps. Bring to a boil, then add potatoes. Reduce heat and simmer 15–18 minutes until potatoes are fully tender and beginning to break down at the edges.

#### 5. Add Corn and Cream
Add reserved corn kernels. Simmer 5 minutes. Stir in cream. Taste and adjust salt and pepper.

## Texture Options

| Style | Technique | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **Chunky** | Leave as-is | Hearty; potato and corn pieces intact |
| **Creamy** | Blend half with an immersion blender | Thick and smooth with some texture |
| **Silky** | Blend fully, then strain | Restaurant-style; very smooth |

## Building the Full Chowder

The base is complete as-is, but common additions:

- **Smoked fish** (smoked salmon, smoked trout) — stir in at the end; don't cook
- **Bacon or pancetta** — render first, use the fat for the base, crumble on top
- **Roasted poblano** — char, peel, dice, and stir in with the corn
- **Bay shrimp** — add in the last 3 minutes; overcooking makes them rubbery

## Key Tips

1. **Don't skip the cob stock** — it's the difference between good and extraordinary
2. **Use Yukon Golds, not russets** — they hold their shape better while still contributing starch
3. **Roux before liquid** — adding flour to hot fat before any liquid prevents lumps
4. **Add cream at the end** — boiling cream after it's added can break the chowder
5. **White pepper over black** — keeps the base pale and the flavor mild
