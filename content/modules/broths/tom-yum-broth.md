# Tom Yum Broth

Tom yum is one of Thailand's defining soups — a broth that is simultaneously sour, spicy, and deeply aromatic. Unlike the Thai coconut broth, tom yum is clear and thin, built on a stock base rather than coconut milk. Its power comes entirely from fresh aromatics and bold seasoning. When the balance is right, it tastes electric.

## Why This Base Works

Tom yum broth relies on **three pillars**:

1. **Lemongrass, galangal, and kaffir lime** — the aromatic backbone
2. **Nam prik pao (roasted chili paste)** — adds smokiness, body, and depth that raw chilies alone don't provide
3. **Fish sauce and lime** — the salt-acid pairing that defines the soup's character

The broth is intentionally light — no cream, no coconut milk, no roux. What makes it substantial is the intensity of flavor packed into a clear liquid.

## Two Styles of Tom Yum

| Style | Thai Name | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Clear** | Tom Yum Goong Nam Sai | Pure broth; ingredients visible; light and sour |
| **Milky/creamy** | Tom Yum Goong Nam Khon | Evaporated milk added at end; richer, slightly milder |

This recipe covers the clear base, which is more versatile. The creamy version is simply a final step: stir in 2–3 tbsp evaporated milk before serving.

## The Base Recipe

**Makes about 5 cups (serves 4)**

### Ingredients

- 4 cups shrimp stock, chicken stock, or water
- 3 stalks lemongrass, bruised and cut into 2-inch pieces
- 1-inch piece galangal, sliced thin
- 6 kaffir lime leaves, torn
- 4–6 Thai bird's eye chilies, bruised (adjust for heat)
- 4 garlic cloves, smashed
- 2 tbsp nam prik pao (roasted chili paste) — see note below
- 3 tbsp fish sauce, plus more to taste
- 2 tbsp fresh lime juice, plus more to taste
- 1 tsp sugar
- 1 cup straw mushrooms or oyster mushrooms, halved
- 2 Roma tomatoes, cut into wedges
- Salt to taste

**To serve:**
- Cilantro leaves
- Spring onions, sliced thin
- Fresh lime wedges
- Additional chilies, if desired

### Method

#### 1. Build the Aromatic Stock
Bring stock to a boil in a medium saucepan. Add lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime leaves, chilies, and garlic. Reduce heat and simmer for 10–12 minutes to infuse the broth fully.

#### 2. Add the Chili Paste
Stir in nam prik pao. It will dissolve into the broth, turning it slightly orange and adding smokiness and body. Simmer 2 minutes.

#### 3. Add Mushrooms and Tomatoes
Add mushrooms and tomato wedges. Simmer 4–5 minutes until mushrooms are tender and tomatoes have softened and begun releasing their juice into the broth.

#### 4. Season
Add fish sauce, lime juice, and sugar. Taste: the broth should be assertively sour, salty, and spicy — bolder than you'd expect, because it will mellow slightly when served. Adjust each element until the balance feels sharp and alive.

#### 5. Serve Immediately
Tom yum is best served right away. Ladle into bowls and top with cilantro, spring onion, and fresh lime wedges.

## Nam Prik Pao — The Key Ingredient

Nam prik pao (roasted chili paste) is sold in jars at Asian grocery stores and is an essential pantry item for Thai cooking. Look for:

- **Mae Pranom** or **Maesri** brands — widely available and reliable
- The paste is dark, oily, and has a complex, smoky-sweet heat

Without it, the broth tastes flat and one-dimensional. In a pinch, substitute 1 tsp smoked paprika + ½ tsp chili flakes, but seek out the real thing.

## Building Soups from This Base

| Protein | Prep | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| **Shrimp (goong)** | Peeled, deveined | 2–3 min; classic tom yum |
| **Chicken (gai)** | Sliced thin | 5–7 min; add after aromatics |
| **Mixed seafood** | Cleaned, cut | 3–4 min; add last |
| **Tofu** | Cubed, firm | 5 min; vegetarian version with soy sauce |
| **Fish (pla)** | Thick fillets | 5–6 min; gentle simmer only |

## Shrimp Stock (Worth Making)

If using shrimp: peel them and simmer the shells in water for 15 minutes with a smashed garlic clove. Strain. This is the traditional base for tom yum goong and dramatically improves the broth.

## The Balance Test

Taste the broth before serving and ask yourself:
- **Too sour?** Add a pinch of sugar or a touch more fish sauce
- **Too salty?** Add more lime juice or a splash of water
- **Too spicy?** Add more stock to dilute, or a spoonful of sugar
- **Flat?** Add more fish sauce and lime together — often both are needed

## Key Tips

1. **Bold seasoning is correct** — tom yum is meant to be intense; timid seasoning produces a bland broth
2. **Don't strain the broth** — the aromatics are left in and set aside by the diner
3. **Serve immediately** — the brightness of fresh lime fades quickly; make and serve
4. **Shrimp stock is the traditional base** — water works, but stock makes a noticeably better broth
5. **Bruise, don't mince** — lemongrass and galangal release more slowly when bruised; mincing makes the broth bitter
