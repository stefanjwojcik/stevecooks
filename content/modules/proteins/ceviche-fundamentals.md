# Ceviche Fundamentals

Ceviche is one of the most elegant no-cook (technically!) dishes in a pescatarian cook's repertoire. Fresh fish is "cooked" by the acidity of citrus juice rather than heat — the acid denatures the proteins in the same way heat does, resulting in firm, flavorful fish.

## How Citrus "Cooks" Fish

When fish is submerged in acid (lime or lemon juice), the citric acid denatures the proteins, turning the flesh from translucent to opaque and firming its texture. This is not the same as killing bacteria the way heat does — which is why using the freshest, highest-quality fish is essential.

## Choosing the Right Fish

The best fish for ceviche is firm, white-fleshed, and very fresh:

- **Sea bass / striped bass** — classic choice, rich flavor
- **Halibut** — firm, slightly sweet
- **Snapper** — popular in Mexican and Peruvian ceviche
- **Tilapia** — affordable and accessible, works well
- **Mahi mahi** — holds up beautifully in acid

**Always use sushi-grade or very fresh fish.** If you can't get truly fresh fish, use previously frozen (commercially frozen fish is often cleaner than "fresh" supermarket fish).

## Basic Ceviche Recipe

**Ingredients (serves 2–3):**
- 1 lb firm white fish, cut into ½-inch cubes
- ¾ cup fresh lime juice (about 6–8 limes)
- ½ small red onion, finely diced
- 1–2 jalapeños or serrano peppers, minced
- ½ cup fresh cilantro, chopped
- Salt to taste

**Optional add-ins:**
- 1 avocado, cubed
- 1 cup cucumber, diced
- 1 cup cherry tomatoes, halved

### Method

1. **Cut** fish into uniform ½-inch cubes — consistent size ensures even curing
2. **Combine** fish and lime juice in a non-reactive bowl (glass or ceramic)
3. **Marinate** for 15–30 minutes for a partially cured texture, or up to 1 hour for fully opaque fish
4. **Add** red onion, peppers, and salt; toss gently
5. **Fold in** cilantro and any optional ingredients just before serving
6. **Taste** and adjust salt and acid

## Regional Variations

### Mexican Ceviche
Tomatoes, cucumber, avocado, and cilantro are common. Often served with tostadas or tortilla chips and a splash of Clamato or hot sauce.

### Peruvian Ceviche (Ceviche de Pescado)
More minimal: fish, lime juice, red onion, aji amarillo (Peruvian yellow pepper), and cilantro. Served with sweet potato and corn (choclo). The tiger's milk (leche de tigre) — the leftover citrus marinade — is prized and sometimes drunk as a shot.

### Shrimp Ceviche
Use cooked or raw shrimp. If raw, cure in lime juice for 30–45 minutes. The result is slightly sweeter and more substantial.

## Timing Guide

| Marination Time | Result |
|---|---|
| 5–10 minutes | Raw center, slightly cured exterior ("aguachile" style) |
| 15–20 minutes | Partially cured, tender and translucent in center |
| 30–45 minutes | Fully cured, opaque throughout |
| 1+ hours | Firm and fully opaque, more cooked texture |

## Key Tips

1. **Use fresh lime juice only** — bottled juice lacks the brightness needed
2. **Non-reactive bowl** — avoid metal; use glass, ceramic, or plastic
3. **Salt at the end** — salt draws moisture, so season just before serving
4. **Serve immediately** — ceviche is best eaten fresh; it continues to cure in the fridge
5. **Keep everything cold** — work with chilled fish and serve over ice if possible
